### Narration craft standard (project-wide)

1. **The 1-second visual buffer.** Never speak the instant a screen changes. Each action cue is followed by `<break time="1.4s" />` so the click and transition land before the explanation begins.

## Timed script

[00:00 | Step 1 — Employee Schedules]
Open Employee Schedules from the sidebar. <break time="1.4s" /> Four tiles read across the top: who's scheduled this week, how many shifts sit open, your coverage percentage, and how many sites that covers. This is your first stop when a manager asks whether the week is staffed.

[00:21 | Step 2 — Schedule grid]
Now the grid itself. <break time="1.2s" /> One row per person, one column per day, Monday through Sunday. Each cell holds a shift code, a start and end like seven to three, or off for a day off. Read across a single row and you have that person's whole week.

[00:43 | Step 3 — Shift Types & Coverage]
Above the grid, two panels do the math for you. <break time="1.2s" /> One splits the week by time of day: morning, afternoon, evening. The other rates coverage, how much is covered, at risk, or still open. You can spot a thin day before you ever open a row.

[01:05 | Step 4 — Time Clock]
Switch to Time Clock. <break time="1.4s" /> Schedules are the plan. This is what actually happened. The tile to watch is On Clock Now, your live count of everyone punched in across the network, right this second.

_[Director: click Time Clock in the sidebar; let the dashboard settle before the line lands.]_

[01:21 | Step 5 — Open punch]
Run your eye down the clock-out column. <break time="1.4s" /> Most rows show a time in and a time out. One shows a dash. That dash isn't missing data. It means the person is still on the clock, and their hours keep climbing until they punch out.

[01:43 | Step 6 — Punch Status]
Off to the side, the punch-status panel. <break time="1.2s" /> It splits today into on the clock, clocked out, and no show. No show is the one to scan for: scheduled staff who never punched in. That's a call worth making before the shift falls short.

[02:04 | Step 7 — Employee Roles]
Open Employee Roles. <break time="1.4s" /> A role is a job's permission set, what that person is allowed to touch. The tiles count your total roles, how many are active, and how many sit at manager or admin level.

[02:22 | Step 8 — Role List]
The list names each role and, in the last column, its access level. <break time="1.2s" /> Administrator has full reach. A cashier gets the point of sale and little else. When someone can't do something they expect to, this column is usually the reason.

[02:43 | Step 9 — Access Levels]
The access-levels panel sums it up. <break time="1.2s" /> Most of your staff sit at the low tier, and that's by design. Far more cashiers and attendants than managers. The panel beside it ranks roles by how many people hold each one.

[03:02 | Step 10 — User Groups]
Last page, User Groups. <break time="1.4s" /> A group is a named set of people, and it's a different idea from a role. A role is about permissions. A group is about who belongs together.

[03:19 | Step 11 — User Group List]
Each group lists its description, its member count, and its locations. <break time="1.2s" /> Some cover a single site, like the M-L-A-S staff group. Others span every location, like all cashiers. The locations column tells you a group's reach at a glance.

[03:38 | Step 12 — Coverage & Maintenance]
One thing that isn't obvious on screen. <break time="1.4s" /> Groups are how Maintenance work gets assigned. Route an issue to the detail team, and it reaches everyone in that group. So hold the split in your head: roles decide what a person can do, groups decide who the work lands on.

[04:00 | Step 13 — Recap]
Four pages, four questions. <break time="1.0s" /> Schedules, for who works which days. Time Clock, for who's punched in right now. Roles, for what someone's allowed to do. Groups, for who belongs to which team. Every one is a list you read, never a screen you edit. Trust the tiles for the live count, read the rows for the detail, and leave the punches and schedules alone.
